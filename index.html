04 · Voice & Tone

Plain-spoken.
Structurally honest.

The organizer who went to medical school. Clarity is a clinical responsibility. The brand does not perform concern — it delivers access. Every word should be something a patient actually wants to read.

Political / Origin Register
"The door was always supposed to be here."
Activates a sense of reclamation — something owed, not granted. Telehealth access as restoration of a right, not a new product feature. Resonates with communities for whom healthcare access was structurally withheld. Works without naming demographics because it speaks to anyone who felt excluded.
Product / Practical Register
"Your health, in your hands. Starting with 20,000 clinicians."
Pairs empowerment language with clinical specificity. "Your hands" = agency + mobile-first access. "20,000 clinicians" grounds it — this isn't aspiration, it's infrastructure. The specificity earns the empowerment. The number is the proof point, not the headline.
Collective Equity Register
"When the people who understand your life own the healthcare platform, everything changes."
The parasocial trust transfer argument made structural. Celebrity ownership as community representation — not just a face on a product, but equity in the platform that serves that community. Directly encodes the Tidal governance model as a patient benefit.
Data / Trust Register
"Same clinician standards. Different waiting room."
Positions telehealth as a form factor change, not a quality compromise. For skeptical audiences who associate telehealth with lower-tier care. The "waiting room" is the barrier being removed; the clinical standard is what stays constant. Short, confident, earned.

Sub-Brand Voice Differentiation
Cardi Care
Unapologetically real. No clinical jargon. Direct, bold, warm. Cardi has always talked openly about her body and health — this is the institutional home for that honesty. "Real talk" means saying what the doctor won't say the way the doctor won't say it. Bilingual fluency is native, not translated.
Yeezy Health
Minimal. Architectural. The infrastructure speaks. "Health is the foundation of everything else." Sentences end when they're done. Negative space carries weight. The brand operates independently of celebrity persona — by design. Walls of copy are a failure state.
King Wellness
Earned authority. Championship mentality applied to everyday health. "The same discipline that built the career builds the body and the mind." Warm but rigorous. Performance for people who aren't professional athletes — they just want to perform at their level. Whatever that level is.
Fenty Wellness
Inclusive precision. The same ethos Fenty Beauty brought to cosmetics — "everyone deserves to see themselves in this." Clinical care that doesn't feel clinical. Luxury that is structural, not exclusive. Care that notices what other systems missed about you. The 40-shade equivalent for healthcare.

08 · Brand Principles

Five commitments.
One equity principle.

These are not aspirational positioning statements. They are operational constraints — the requirements the brand holds itself to, and holds every partner, celebrity, and clinician to. If any of these are violated, the brand is violated.

01
Collective
Individual health is a collective act. The conditions that make a person sick or well are shared — housing, environment, access, economic stress. COMMONGROUND operates from this understanding, not despite it. Programs are designed for individual care. The system is designed for collective impact. These are not in conflict.
→ 38× telehealth utilization above pre-pandemic baseline · Sustained and structural, not temporary
02
Access
Access is a design constraint, not a feature. It is not added at the end and it is not a "social impact" layer. When access is the starting point — mobile-first, language-concordant, payer-inclusive — the entire product changes. Hispanic adults have the highest telehealth adoption rate of any demographic because mobile-first telehealth closed a physical access gap. That gap was a design failure. We don't repeat it.
→ 16.7% Hispanic telehealth adoption (highest) · 14.6% Black · 15.8% AAPI · The SPEAK Act (2026) codified language-concordant care
03
Clinicians
The clinician is the brand. The celebrity opens the door. The clinician keeps it open. Parasocial trust gets a patient to make an appointment. Clinical trust keeps them engaged for 6–14 months. Every COMMONGROUND sub-brand is only as good as the clinicians who deliver care within it. 20,000+ credentialed providers is not a statistic — it is the product. Function Health, Midi Health, Ro — the platforms that lasted are the ones that understood this.
→ 62.6% telemental health visit volume increase 2018–2024 · Clinician-delivered, not algorithm-delivered
04
Plain Language
Clarity is a clinical responsibility. A patient who doesn't understand their diagnosis, their program, or their options cannot meaningfully consent to care. COMMONGROUND brands write for the patient, not for the insurance form. Every piece of copy — from the app to the clinician's follow-up message — gets the plain language treatment. Not dumbed down. Translated. There is a difference.
→ 44% mental health service gap for Hispanic adults attributable in part to language barriers · Plain language is clinical infrastructure
05
Infrastructure
Clinical rigor is the floor, not the ceiling. FDA enforcement cleared the market of platforms that treated compliance as optional. That enforcement is a permanent market condition now, not a temporary crackdown. Platforms that survived and grew were the ones with genuine clinical infrastructure. OpenLoop Health's "frugal, compliance-first" operating model is the foundation every sub-brand is built on. The infrastructure is the moat. The brand is the door.
→ FDA enforcement surge 2025 eliminated low-credibility platforms · DEA remote prescribing extended through 2026 · Medicare telehealth through 2027
06
Equity,
Not
Endorsement
Every celebrity partner is a co-founder with equity, commitment milestones, and skin in the outcome. Not a face on a package. Not a check at the end of a campaign. This is what the Tidal model looked like for music in 2015 — Jay-Z didn't hire Beyoncé and Rihanna to post about Tidal, he made them equity holders with a stake in what was built. Sprint invested $200M at a $600M valuation because the artist-equity model was credible. The Triller cautionary tale showed what happens when celebrity engagement operates without mission alignment: $1.14B in losses in 2024. COMMONGROUND is the healthcare version of what Tidal was for music, and what United Artists was for cinema in 1919 — creators wresting control of the platform from the institutions that had always owned it. This is what that looks like for healthcare in 2026. The first deal that makes a celebrity a founder, not a face. That framing is the brand.
→ Tidal: 2015 · Equity to Beyoncé, Rihanna, Jay-Z + 13 artists · Sprint: $200M at $600M val · United Artists: 1919 · COMMONGROUND: 2026
